Simple Pantry Tomato Sauce

Description

Is there anything simpler, more satisfying than a good plate of pasta? I'd certainly be hard-pressed to name anything. Maybe a plate of pasta that I didn't have to do anything to create, but that's neither here nor there. This is a recipe website, so you're still going to have to actually do the work to produce these dishes. Unless you're the type of person who can just email your personal assistant a recipe you want to try, and by the time you get home from a hard day on the set of the new blockbuster superhero epic, your chef has been slaving over a hot stove all day to produce whatever delectable dish your heart desires. But that probably isn't your vibe.

I digress. The point is: a simple, well-made pasta sauce is one of life's great joys, and I happen to have one that could bring a tear to your eye—that is, if you didn't already waste all your tears shooting the second-act breakup scene with Jennifer Aniston.

When it comes to a good, and I really mean good, not just Olive Garden-good, pasta sauce, there is one secret ingredient that trumps all others: time. That's right: the good old passage of second, minutes, and, crucially for this recipe, hours. This particular sauce doesn't even hit its stride until the second hour rolls around, but ideally you should shoot for three. I know that sounds like a tall order in this hustle-bustle-TikTok-ADHD world of flying cars and 3-second viral videos, but if you really want to make a tomato sauce you can be proud of, you're going to have to put in the time.

And notice I said "put in the time," not "put in the work." This recipe is "Simple," like it says at the top of the screen. Despite the amount of time it takes to cook this sauce, the actual work you have to put in is fairly minimal, and most of the ingredients are things you already have in your kitchen. You do a little work up front, then you get to, as Ron Popeil once said, set it and forget it. And three hours later, (well, maybe a bit longer than three hours*) you've got yourself what I promise will be one of the best sauces you've ever tasted—OR YOUR MONEY BACK!


* There is a lot of discussion in the foodie community how and how long flavors take to develop. A lot of people, notably J. Kenji Lopez-Alt, the brilliant food scientist of America's Test Kitchen, New York Times Cooking, and Serious Eats fame, insist that the flavors within stews—in this case a pasta sauce, but really anything cooked in liquid in a pot for a long time, including traditional stews and chili—take a long time to mature. In his excellent cookbook/textbook The Food Lab, Lopez-Alt recommends not even eating chili the same day you make it. He says to turn off the heat, let it come to room temp, put it in the fridge, and wait overnight at minimum before enjoying. Heck, he actually says it can take up to a week for the flavors to fully develop.

Now while I would trust Kenji with my life on all things culinary, sometimes you have to follow your gut, not your brain. I say three hours and straight on the plate is plenty fine for this pasta sauce. Do I think there is an appreciable difference in flavor after the sauce has cooled? Yes. I'd be lying if I said otherwise. My honest recommendation would be to simmer for 3 hours and leave the pot sitting on the stove at least long enough to come down to room temp. This definitely improves the flavor, and not burning the hell out of your tongue is a big bonus.

But! I realize that being in a house filled with the savory aromas of stewing tomatoes, garlic, and aromatics for 3-plus hours, especially on a cold winter afternoon, can create a hunger stronger than the strongest smart-guy lecture about phenols and tannins and culinary chemical reactions, Maillard and otherwise. The heart stomach know what the heart stomach wants. If you can't wait another second to eat this delicious, hearty, stick-to-your-ribs-and-your-spoon pasta sauce, you go right ahead. There will still be sauce left for tomorrow, and you can rest easy knowing that it will taste even better then!

Ingredients

  • Two 28oz cans whole peeled tomatoes (any variety will do)
  • 2 medium onions, finely chopped
  • 10 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2½ tbsp dried basil
  • 1 tsp dried oregano
  • 1 tbsp dried parsley
  • ½ tsp red pepper flakes
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 1 tsp white sugar
  • about 1-2 tbsp tomato paste
  • 2 large anchovies, finely chopped
  • 2 tbsp butter
  • ¾ cups water
  • olive oil

Steps

  1. Preheat oven to 300° and move rack to lower-middle so that a dutch oven will fit with its lid on. Crush canned tomatoes by hand in a large bowl, into roughly ½ inch pieces. Be careful, as the tomatoes have a tendency to squirt liquid when crushed.
  2. In a dutch oven, heat olive oil (about 2 tbsp) on medium-high. Cook onion until softened, about 3 minutes. Add garlic, anchovies, and tomato paste, cook until fragrant, about 2 minutes. Add basil, red pepper flakes, salt, and sugar. Stir to combine. Add butter and cook for one more minute.
  3. Add half the tomatoes and deglaze the dutch oven (cook on high for about 30 seconds while scraping the bottom of the pot with a wooden spoon). Add remaining tomatoes and water. Bring back up to medium heat and bring to a simmer.
  4. Transfer dutch oven to preheated oven. Cover, leaving lid slightly ajar. Cook 2-3 hours, removing dutch oven every 30 minutes to stir and make sure sauce is not burning. Make sure the sauce is still at a simmer before returning to oven.
  5. After 3 hours, move dutch oven to stove or countertop to cool, uncovered, until it has reached room temperature. At this point, you can re-warm the sauce and serve, or move to refrigerator overnight.
